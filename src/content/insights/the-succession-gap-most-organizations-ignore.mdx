Ask most leadership teams whether they have a succession plan and the answer is yes. Ask them what's actually in it, and you'll usually get a list of names. Someone is tagged as "ready now." Someone else is tagged as "18 months." A third name appears on the list because someone thought they should. The document is updated once a year, before the board conversation, and filed.

That is a succession inventory. It is not a succession system.

The distinction matters because the inventory approach has a specific failure mode: when a key leader exits unexpectedly, the person tagged "ready" either isn't — or is, but lands in a role they weren't genuinely prepared for because preparation was assumed, not designed. The organization scrambles. The successor wobbles. The talent pipeline looks thin, even though names were on the list.

The organizations that navigate transitions well share three conditions that rarely show up in the inventory.

## Psychological safety to stretch before the moment arrives

The first condition is one most succession frameworks miss entirely: the identified person has to be able to fail, visibly, in front of people who matter — before the high-stakes transition. If the culture only rewards certainty and polished execution, successors learn to stay in their lane. They don't build the range that leadership transitions require.

What this looks like in practice: a VP given a board presentation to lead, with coaching before and a debrief after. A high-potential Director asked to represent the executive team in a negotiation they'd normally only be briefed on. A manager given explicit permission to run a cross-functional initiative with a real budget and real stakes.

None of this needs to be labeled "succession development." It just needs to happen — consistently, with someone watching, and with a safe enough container for honest feedback afterward.

## Cross-functional exposure before it's needed

The second condition is structural. Leaders who navigate transitions effectively have almost always worked across the organization before the transition — not in it, not adjacent to it, but genuinely embedded in a different function for a sustained period.

This is harder to engineer than it sounds. Finance doesn't want to lend their high performer to operations for six months. Operations doesn't have bandwidth to properly integrate someone from finance. So the exposure doesn't happen, and the successor arrives in a broad leadership role with a narrow functional worldview.

The organizations that get this right treat cross-functional exposure as a formal commitment, not an informal hope. They name it as a development expectation. They set it up structurally — secondments, project sponsorships, cross-functional task forces with real authority — and they protect it from the short-term pressures that would otherwise crowd it out.

## Sponsor relationships, not just mentors

The third condition is relational — and it's the one most commonly confused. Mentors give advice. Sponsors spend capital. A mentor tells you what to work on. A sponsor puts your name in rooms you're not in and advocates for the opportunity you're ready for.

Most succession plans are heavy on mentorship and light on sponsorship. The person tagged "ready now" has a mentor. Whether they have a senior advocate who knows their work well enough to stake reputation on it is a different question — and usually the more predictive one.

Sponsorship is particularly scarce for people who don't look like the leadership team they're moving into. It doesn't appear on its own. It has to be named, structured, and held accountable.

## The diagnostic question

If you're honest about your own succession system, ask this: If three of your most senior people left in the next 90 days — not one, but three — do you have people who could step into expanded scope, with the conditions to succeed, starting Monday?

Not names on a list. People with stretch experience behind them, cross-functional fluency, and at least one senior advocate who knows their work well enough to say: this person is ready.

If the honest answer is no, the gap isn't talent. It's the system.
