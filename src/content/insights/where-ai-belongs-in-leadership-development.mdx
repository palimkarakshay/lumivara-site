A leadership team I worked with last quarter was about to deploy an AI coaching tool to all 280 managers. The pilot results looked great: high engagement, strong satisfaction, "more coaching conversations than we've ever had." On paper, a clear win.

I asked one question: *"Of the conversations the tool surfaced, how many ended in a real change of behavior — by the manager or the report?"*

Nobody had measured that. It hadn't occurred to anyone to measure it. The tool produced conversations, the conversations produced satisfaction, satisfaction produced enthusiasm. The chain stopped there.

This is the moment we're in with AI in people work. The tools are good. The use cases are real. And it's increasingly easy to mistake activity for impact.

## What AI is genuinely good at

Three things, in my experience, where AI moves the needle in leadership and learning:

**1. Coaching-at-scale, with caveats.**
A first-time manager in a 200-person company has a real problem: their manager doesn't have time to coach them, their HR business partner is stretched, and the formal program runs once a quarter. An AI coaching tool that lets them think out loud, get a structured prompt, surface what they're avoiding — that's genuinely valuable. It doesn't replace human coaching; it fills the gap on Tuesday at 4pm when the human isn't available.

**2. Personalized development.**
Generic learning paths are a known failure mode. "Here's the leadership curriculum, work through it" produces low completion and lower retention. AI can do the boring work of mapping a person's specific gaps to the specific resources that close them — and re-doing that mapping every two months as the person grows. Done well, this is a real lift.

**3. Content and material generation.**
Drafting a workshop facilitator guide that used to take a senior L&D person two days now takes two hours of generation plus three hours of review. The savings are real. The output is usually better, because the human time goes into editing and judgment instead of starting from blank.

## What AI is not good at — and probably won't be

Three things where AI cannot meaningfully replace the human work:

**1. Feedback that lands.**
The hardest part of feedback is not generating it. It's delivering it in a relationship where the receiver trusts that the feedback is meant to make them better, not to position the giver. AI can draft feedback. It cannot establish trust. And feedback without trust gets received as criticism — usually defensively, sometimes destructively.

**2. Modeling values.**
Leadership culture is set, primarily, by what senior leaders *do* under stress. Whether they tell the truth when it's costly. Whether they take responsibility when something fails. Whether they protect the team when there's pressure from above. No AI tool will model that for your organization. Only your senior leaders will, and the rest of the system reads them constantly.

**3. The room.**
Some of the most consequential leadership development happens in the unstructured time around formal sessions — the hallway conversation, the dinner where someone admits what they're actually struggling with, the small group debrief that turns into something more honest. AI doesn't generate the room. It doesn't replace the room. And if you optimize so heavily for AI-mediated learning that you remove the room, you'll have lost the part that mattered most.

## A practical framing

Here's the question I now ask every team considering an AI investment in leadership or learning:

> *"What does this tool let us stop doing — and is that thing worth stopping?"*

If the answer is "it lets us stop scheduling so many coaching sessions," that may be a good trade or a bad one, depending on whether those sessions were producing change. If the answer is "it lets us stop having to think about which manager needs what development," that's almost always a bad trade — you're outsourcing the judgment that should sit with the human leaders.

The right use of AI in this work is to remove friction from the things humans should be doing more of, and to take over the things humans were never going to do well anyway. The wrong use is to substitute for the things only humans can do.

## What we actually recommend

When an organization asks me where to start with AI in leadership development, the order looks like this:

1. **Start with content workflows.** Lowest risk, highest immediate ROI, and you learn how your team works with AI-generated material. That's the prerequisite for everything else.
2. **Layer in personalized recommendations.** Once you have a working capability framework, AI can do the matching work that nobody had time to do manually.
3. **Pilot AI coaching narrowly.** A specific cohort, a specific use case, a specific success metric. Do not roll it out enterprise-wide on the strength of a satisfaction score.
4. **Never substitute it for the senior-leadership behavioral work.** That has to remain human, and visibly so.

AI is a lever in this work — a real one. Levers that aren't used carefully break things. The trick is not avoiding the lever. It's being honest about which loads it can carry and which it can't.

That's the line we hold for clients. It's the same line we hold for our own practice.
