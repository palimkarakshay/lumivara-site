Organizations between 50 and 200 people share a specific HR problem that larger companies don't have and smaller ones don't yet face: they've outgrown informal people management but haven't built enough of a recurring HR workload to justify the full-time headcount to do it properly.

This is where the two classic failure modes appear.

**The under-hire.** A growing tech company at 80 people realizes it needs HR. It posts for a generalist, finds someone good at administration but not at strategy, and six months later wonders why compensation bands are finally documented but manager capability hasn't moved. The hire was technically right. The scope wasn't.

**The over-hire.** A professional services firm at 120 people brings on a senior HR leader at a cost that makes sense in a 300-person organization. She's excellent. She also runs out of meaningful work inside six months. She leaves. The firm is back to square one, now more skeptical of the function.

Both failure modes trace to the same root: the organization hired a full-time person for a part-time or episodic need. The solution isn't a better hire. It's a different model.

## When fractional HR is the right call

Fractional HR works best when three conditions align.

**A specific initiative, not ambient operations.** If what you actually need is a performance management redesign, a manager training program, a compensation audit, or a hiring process overhaul, that's a defined project with a beginning, middle, and end. A full-time hire will complete the project in three months and then spend nine months looking for work to justify the position. A fractional engagement gets the work done and exits cleanly.

**A defined scope with clear ownership.** Fractional HR fails when it's deployed as a catch-all. "We need HR" is not a scope. "We need someone to run our H1 performance cycle and train our eight new managers" is. The clearer the mandate, the more value the fractional model delivers. Ambiguous scope turns fractional into expensive limbo — too much to be a consultant, not enough to be an employee.

**A window before you're ready to hire full-time.** Many organizations use fractional HR as a bridge: they know they'll want a full-time HR leader eventually, but they're not at the headcount, complexity, or budget level where that hire makes sense yet. A fractional engagement in this window is genuinely useful — it builds the HR infrastructure, establishes the function's credibility with the leadership team, and often helps define what the eventual full-time hire should look like.

## When full-time is the right call

Fractional is not always the answer. If the organization has reached a sustained, high-frequency HR workload — complex employee relations issues ongoing, frequent regulatory demands, a large manager population that needs consistent coaching and support — a full-time presence usually outperforms a fractional one. Fractional practitioners, however skilled, can't provide the institutional continuity that a complex, active people function requires.

The signal to watch for: if you're finding yourself constantly bringing the fractional person up to speed on evolving situations, or if the work is recurring and fast-moving rather than project-shaped, the fractional model is working against you.

## The questions worth asking before you post the job

Before you write the job description, run this framework:

1. **What specifically is broken, and is it project work or steady-state work?** List the three things most wrong with your HR function right now. Are they initiatives (discrete projects with an end state) or operational rhythms (recurring processes that need sustained ownership)?

2. **How will we use this person's time in month seven?** If you can't answer this clearly, the full-time scope isn't there yet.

3. **What does success look like at six months, and is that a reasonable amount of work for one person for six months?** If success is achievable in three months, you don't need a permanent hire to achieve it.

4. **Are we hiring to solve a problem or to have HR covered?** These are different motivations with different right answers.

The organizations that make fractional HR work treat it as a deliberate choice with a defined mandate — not a compromise or a holding pattern. When it's structured that way, it consistently delivers more value per dollar than the full-time hire that came too early.
