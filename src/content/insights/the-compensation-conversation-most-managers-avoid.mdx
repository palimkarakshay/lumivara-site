Ontario's pay transparency legislation requires employers to include salary ranges in publicly posted job ads. The compliance requirement is clear, the implementation is straightforward, and most organizations have figured out the mechanics. What they haven't figured out is what comes next: the conversation.

When your existing employees see a salary range posted for a role similar to theirs — or, in some cases, for their exact role — they will have questions. Not hypothetically. They will have them, and they will bring them to their managers. The organizations preparing only for the compliance piece are going to be surprised by the human piece.

## The three questions employees actually ask

Pay transparency legislation surfaces three questions that have always existed but were previously easier to defer.

**Am I paid fairly relative to new hires?** When a posted range is visible, and it overlaps with or exceeds what a current employee earns, the question isn't abstract anymore. It's specific and personal. Employees who have been loyal, developed in the role, and contributed over time feel the comparison acutely — and they want to know whether their tenure counted for anything.

**Do I understand how my pay was determined?** Most employees know their salary. Very few know how it was set. What band are they in? What moved them up within the band? What would it take to reach the top of the band? When transparency makes ranges visible, the question of methodology follows. An answer of "it's complicated" won't hold.

**Is the manager equipped to have this conversation with me?** This is the implicit question, and it's the one that most predicts the outcome. A manager who answers with confidence, with actual information, and with a clear framework for what's possible preserves the relationship. A manager who deflects, goes vague, or apologizes without information erodes it — often irreparably.

## The real challenge is manager readiness

Organizations tend to treat pay transparency as a compensation problem. Define the bands, post the ranges, update the HRIS. The harder work is preparing the people who will field the questions — typically line managers who are one or two levels away from the compensation decisions, didn't set the bands, don't fully understand the methodology, and have never been coached on how to have this conversation.

Left unprepared, managers default to one of three bad patterns: they go vague ("that's not really something I can speak to"), they over-promise ("I'll look into getting you adjusted"), or they go silent and hope the employee doesn't follow up. All three damage trust in the manager specifically and in the organization generally.

The conversation managers actually need to be able to have requires three things: an honest explanation of where the employee sits in the band and why, a clear description of what would move them within or beyond it, and a consistent message about timelines and what they can and can't commit to. None of this requires the manager to have set the compensation philosophy. It requires them to understand it well enough to represent it.

## A framework for the conversation

When an employee raises the compensation question — whether in a scheduled review or in an impromptu hallway moment — the manager needs to be able to do four things:

1. **Acknowledge the question directly.** "That's a fair thing to be thinking about" is a better opening than "I can't really speak to that."

2. **Orient the employee to the band.** Where do they sit? What does the band represent? Not every detail needs to be shared, but the employee should leave the conversation with a clearer picture than they had.

3. **Explain the path.** What would move someone within or beyond this band? Is it performance, scope expansion, or a combination? If the answer is "it would require a role change," say that clearly rather than leaving false hope.

4. **Be honest about what's possible and when.** If a compensation review cycle is coming, say so. If the timeline is uncertain, say that instead. Ambiguity managed well is better than false precision.

The organizations that navigate compensation transparency well aren't the ones with the most sophisticated bands. They're the ones whose managers were actually prepared to use them in real conversations.
