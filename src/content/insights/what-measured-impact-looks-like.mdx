"Measured by impact" is one of those phrases that sounds settled but isn't. Walk into ten organizations and ask how they measure the impact of their people work, and you'll get ten different answers — most of them lagging, most of them confounded with other variables, most of them reported quarterly when the real decisions need to happen weekly.

Here's the version of it I've found actually works.

## Three tiers of measurement

Every people initiative — leadership program, hiring system overhaul, engagement effort — should be measured at three layers. Get one wrong and the others won't tell you the truth.

### Tier 1: Adoption and signal

What you measure in the first 30–60 days. Did people show up? Did they engage? Did the system actually run as designed?

This is not glamorous data. Attendance, completion rates, response rates, time-to-action. But without it, the rest is meaningless — you can't claim a leadership program changed behavior if half the cohort didn't finish it.

### Tier 2: Behavior change

What you measure at 60–120 days. Did the work change *what people do*? This is where most measurement programs collapse, because behavior change is harder to instrument than satisfaction. But there are concrete proxies for almost everything:

- For a leadership program: how managers spend the first 30 minutes of their week (1:1 cadence, agenda quality, follow-through on commitments).
- For a hiring system overhaul: interview consistency scores, time-to-decision, hiring manager confidence in the panel.
- For an engagement initiative: pulse-survey movement on the specific dimension targeted.

If Tier 1 looks great and Tier 2 doesn't move, you have a learning problem, not a people problem. The intervention isn't landing in real work.

### Tier 3: Business outcome

What you measure at 6+ months. The thing leadership actually cares about: retention, performance, revenue per head, customer outcome, time-to-value. These are real measures of impact, but they're slow, noisy, and confounded by everything else happening in the business.

The mistake is to measure *only* Tier 3. By the time the number moves, you've spent a year, and you have no diagnostic for why it did or didn't.

## Why vanity metrics persist

The reason "program NPS = 9.2" keeps showing up in board decks is that it's available, defensible, and feels like proof. It's none of those things. NPS measures whether people enjoyed a thing. It does not measure whether they're now doing the thing differently. The two correlate weakly at best.

Same problem with engagement surveys reported only at the aggregate. A 2-point drop on the overall score tells you nothing — the answer is always inside the sub-dimensions, the manager-level breakdowns, and the comments. Aggregate scores let you say "engagement is up." They don't let you say *why*, and *why* is the only thing that helps you act.

## What we recommend on every engagement

Three commitments, defined in writing before any work starts:

1. **One Tier-1 metric per major activity** — measured weekly, visible to whoever is accountable. Aimed at adoption and signal.
2. **Two Tier-2 metrics for the cohort** — measured at 60 and 120 days. Aimed at behavior change. Tied to the specific outcome the work is supposed to drive.
3. **One Tier-3 metric for the business** — measured at 6 and 12 months. Tied to the leadership question that prompted the engagement in the first place.

Four metrics. Three time horizons. One page. That's it.

If you can't write that page in week one, the engagement isn't yet scoped tightly enough to know whether it worked. That's not a measurement problem — it's a problem-definition problem, and it's solvable in another week of conversation.

## A note on attribution

A reasonable objection: "we can't isolate our leadership program from the comp adjustment we made the same quarter." Correct. You can't, and pretending you can with a regression won't help. What you can do is make the connection plausible, well-instrumented, and judged in good faith by people close enough to the work to know.

That's not the precision a clinical trial would demand. It's the precision a working business actually uses to make resourcing decisions. And it's what leadership teams want from people work — not certainty, but enough signal to know whether to keep going, course-correct, or stop.

## The shorter version

Define what would count as success in writing, before you start. Measure adoption first, behavior next, outcomes last. Don't confuse program enjoyment with program effectiveness. Be honest about attribution.

That's measured impact. The rest is decoration.
