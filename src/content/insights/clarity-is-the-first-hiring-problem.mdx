When a leadership team tells me they have a "talent problem," I stay curious. The label sounds clean — there's a market, the market has people in it, and we're somehow not hiring them well enough. Spend an hour in the actual hiring system, though, and the picture usually shifts. The pipeline is fine. What's broken is upstream of it.

Most hiring, performance, and retention issues I've worked on trace back to one of three clarity gaps:

1. **Unclear roles.** Two people in the same role have different mandates. Two managers describe success differently. The job posting promises one thing; the day-to-day delivers another.
2. **Unclear success criteria.** Performance is judged by impressions, not by a small set of outcomes that everyone agreed to upfront.
3. **Unclear decision rights.** Hiring panels disagree because no one has named who decides what. The "we'll align" never happens because alignment requires a defined arbiter.

Each of those is a clarity problem masquerading as a people problem. And each one is fixable in weeks, not quarters — but only after it's named.

## What this looks like in a real hiring loop

A growing org wants to bring on a Director of Engineering. The first three searches stall: top candidates withdraw, internal disagreement on shortlists, two offers extended and rejected. The leadership lens is *"the market is tight."* Sometimes it is. More often, the market is fine and the role isn't.

Three quick checks usually surface the truth:

- **Are the must-haves consistent across interviewers?** If you asked each panelist to rank the top three competencies, would they agree?
- **Is the success picture written down?** What does "great in this role at the 90-day mark" look like? Can the hiring manager describe it in two sentences?
- **Who decides?** When the panel splits, who gets the deciding vote? Is that person on the panel?

If any of these wobble, the search will keep stalling — even if the candidates are excellent — because the system is making a different decision every week.

## The five-question diagnostic

When I'm trying to get past "we have a talent problem" and into the real picture, I ask:

1. Could you write the top three outcomes for this role on one page, today, without consulting anyone?
2. Would the hiring manager and the next-level-up describe success the same way?
3. Does your interview structure test for those outcomes — or does it test for general intelligence and likeability?
4. When two interviewers disagree, what's the protocol?
5. If the new hire underperforms at six months, do you know whether it's a hire-quality issue or a role-clarity issue?

If three or more of these don't have crisp answers, the gap isn't in talent. It's in clarity. And that's good news, because clarity gaps are designable.

## Why this matters more as you scale

Founder-led organizations get away with implicit clarity because the founder is in every room. The minute you cross 50, 100, 200 people, implicit becomes invisible. New managers inherit roles they didn't define. Goals cascade through three layers and lose their edges. The system keeps running, but on momentum — and momentum can hide a clarity gap for a quarter or two before it shows up as a hiring miss, a flat performance review cycle, or a surprising resignation.

The work isn't glamorous: write the role definitions down. Define what success looks like in measurable terms. Name decision-makers. Then let the hiring system do its job.
