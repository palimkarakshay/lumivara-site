# 01 — Gig Profile (Upwork, Fiverr, Contra, LinkedIn)

The pitch a non-technical small-business owner needs to read. Tech words removed wherever the same point can be made in plain English.

---

## Part 1 — Your one-line positioning

> **I build websites for small businesses that don't fall apart between updates. You can edit yours from your phone, and a small monthly fee keeps it improving in the background.**

That single sentence is the whole offer. Memorise it. Open every conversation with it. Put a version of it on every platform.

Variants by audience:

- **For solo consultants / coaches / therapists / accountants:**  
  "I build clean, fast websites for solo practitioners — and you can update yours by sending a text from your phone. No more waiting weeks for a developer to fix a typo."
- **For small services firms (5–25 staff):**  
  "Your website was built in 2019, your intern can't update it, and your last invoice from the agency was eight hundred dollars for a paragraph change. I solve all three of those problems with one service."
- **For indie founders / SaaS:**  
  "Marketing site that ships in two weeks, runs on modern tooling you can hack on later, and updates itself from a phone shortcut while you focus on the product."

## Part 2 — What you're actually selling (in plain English)

You have three things to sell. Most freelancers can sell one. Lead with whichever one the prospect asked about; mention the others as upgrades.

### Service A — *The website itself*

A clean, fast, mobile-friendly marketing site for the business — usually 3 to 7 pages. Custom design (not a template), proper search-engine setup, accessibility built in, and the client owns everything (the code, the domain, the hosting account). Hand-over takes 30 minutes; they're never locked in.

**What the client experiences:** "I got a beautiful site that loads instantly, ranks well, and looks like my brand — not someone else's template."

### Service B — *The "edit-from-your-phone" system* (this is your moat)

After the site is live, the client gets a tiny app on their phone. They tap it, type the change they want ("Update the price on the consulting page to $300", "Add a new article called X", "Fix the typo in the footer"), and within a few hours they get a notification with a preview link. They tap the link, see the change, tap a button to publish. The website is updated. Their developer (you) was never paged.

**What the client experiences:** "I sent a text from the airport. By the time I landed, it was done. I never opened a laptop."

### Service C — *The "always-improving" subscription*

Every month, the client gets:
- A small backlog of improvements you've spotted on their site (loading speed, accessibility, SEO).
- The phone-edit system kept running, monitored, and patched if anything breaks.
- A monthly check-in so the site grows with the business instead of decaying.

**What the client experiences:** "I'm not paying for a build that gets stale. I'm paying for a website that gets quietly better every month."

## Part 5 — Three gigs to launch with

Don't list more than three. Fiverr's algorithm rewards focused profiles. Upwork prospects skim — three sharp gigs read as expertise; seven blurry ones read as desperation.

### Gig 1 — The headline gig (your differentiator)

**Title:**  
*I will build a small-business website you can update from your phone, with monthly auto-improvements*

**Category:** Programming & Tech → Website Development → Business Websites

**One-paragraph description:**

> Most small business websites die a slow death because every tiny edit needs a developer. Yours won't. I'll build you a fast, beautiful 3–7 page marketing site, then set up a system that lets you submit edits from your phone in 30 seconds. A bot implements them, sends you a preview, you tap publish from your phone. Your laptop can be off the entire time.
>
> What you get: a custom-designed site, a 5-minute training video, full ownership (your domain, your code, your hosting), and the option to keep me on retainer for monthly improvements at a flat fee.

(Detailed package table lives in `02-pricing-tiers.md`.)

### Gig 2 — The volume gig (for buyers who want the site, not the system)

**Title:**  
*I will build a fast, accessible business website with perfect Google rankings*

This is the same craft, smaller scope: just the website, no phone-edit system. Some prospects don't yet understand the value of the autopilot piece — don't lose them. Sell them the site, deliver beautifully, then offer the autopilot as a 30-day post-launch upsell. About 1 in 4 will say yes within six months once they've felt the cost of editing manually.

### Gig 3 — The retainer-only gig (for people who already have a site)

**Title:**  
*I will set up phone-based editing and monthly improvements for your existing website*

For prospects who already have a Next.js or React site (or are willing to migrate). Smaller setup fee, focuses entirely on the autopilot system. Strategic role: gives you a foot in the door at agencies and indie SaaS founders who don't need a new build but love the idea of the maintenance system.

## Part 6 — Universal FAQ (paste on every gig)

> **Q: What information do you need from me to start?**  
> Business name, who you serve, your top action you want visitors to take (book a call, get a quote, buy something), three words for your brand voice, three words for your visual mood, and a list of pages you want. If you don't have a logo or brand colours yet, we'll work them out together. I send a 5-minute intake form when you order.

> **Q: Do I need to know anything technical to use the phone-edit system?**  
> No. It's one shortcut on your phone. Type the change, tap send, get a notification when it's ready, tap publish. I record a personalised walkthrough video when we hand over.

> **Q: Who owns the site after we're done?**  
> You do — completely. The code, the domain, the hosting account all live under your name. I'm just the builder. If you ever want to switch developers or take it in-house, you can on day one.

> **Q: What if the bot makes a mistake on my site?**  
> The bot only proposes changes — it never publishes directly. Every change waits for you to tap "publish" from your phone. It's also explicitly forbidden from touching critical parts of the site without your sign-off. Worst case, you ignore the notification and nothing happens to your live site.

> **Q: How much does the monthly subscription cost to run, behind the scenes?**  
> The monthly fee I charge covers everything — my time, the AI services, the monitoring. There are no surprise add-ons. You will never get a $200 invoice for a one-line change.

> **Q: Will my site look like every other modern website?**  
> No. I design custom palettes, type, and layouts per project. I'll send you a moodboard for sign-off before any code is written.

> **Q: Can you help with logos, branding, or copywriting?**  
> Logos and full branding: no — I'll refer you to specialists I trust. Copy: I'll polish what you give me and write structural placeholders, but I don't ghostwrite from scratch.

> **Q: What's your response time?**  
> Eastern Time (Toronto). I respond to messages within 4 business hours, usually faster.

> **Q: What if I'm not happy with the result?**  
> Each package includes a defined number of revisions. If we use them up and you're still not happy, I'd rather refund the unused portion than ship something neither of us is proud of.

> **Q: Can I see live examples of your work?**  
> Yes — my own consulting site, lumivara.com, was built and is run on the exact same system I'm offering. The fact that I use it myself is the strongest proof I can give you.

## Part 7 — Order requirements form (set these as required on Fiverr)

Setting these stops the order clock until the buyer fills them in — protects your delivery deadline.

1. Business name and one-line description.
2. Who is the website for? (your target customer)
3. What's the *one* thing you want a visitor to do? (book a call, request a quote, buy a product…)
4. Brand voice — three adjectives.
5. Visual mood — three adjectives.
6. List of pages you want (e.g., home, services, about, contact).
7. Three reference websites you like the look of, with one sentence each on what you like.
8. (Optional file upload) Logo, brand colours, fonts.
9. (Optional file upload) Existing copy or content.
10. Your domain name (or "I haven't bought one yet").

## Part 10 — First 30 days: a tight launch plan

**Week 1**
- Set up Fiverr profile + Gig 1 (use copy from Parts 3–7).
- Apply to Toptal, Arc.dev, Lemon.io. Allow 2–4 weeks for screening.
- Record a 30-second portrait video for the Fiverr gig page (Fiverr boosts gigs with video).

**Week 2**
- Soft launch: drop Gig 1's basic tier 15% for the first 5 orders only. Overdeliver on those — they become your review base.
- Apply to 5 Upwork jobs/day with custom proposals; mention the phone-edit system in the first paragraph.

**Week 3**
- Publish Gigs 2 and 3.
- Post a "case study" on LinkedIn + X linking to your live Lumivara site as proof of concept.
- DM 10 people from your existing network with a single paragraph: *"I've built a service that does X — you'd be a perfect first client because Y. Want a 15-min walkthrough?"*

**Week 4**
- Decline scope-creep buyers politely and quickly. One bad review tanks two weeks of momentum on a new gig.
- Once you have 5 reviews on Gig 1, raise its prices 15%. At 10 reviews, raise another 15%.

## Part 11 — What NOT to do

- **Don't price the autopilot tier under USD $1,000.** Low prices attract low-paying buyers and signal low quality.
- **Don't list anything ABAP / SAP-related on Fiverr.** That market is dominated by international sellers at $25/hr — wrong channel for you. Run that capability on Upwork / Toptal / Dice instead.
- **Don't accept off-platform payment in your first 90 days.** Fiverr ToS violation; it'll get a new account banned.
- **Don't promise turnaround times you can't keep.** On-time delivery is the single biggest ranking factor on Fiverr.
- **Don't agree to "just one small change" after delivery.** Out-of-scope changes become custom offers with their own price. Boundaries are how you stay sane.
- **Don't lead with the tech stack.** The prospect doesn't care about Next.js or Tailwind. They care that their site updates from a phone and gets better every month.

---

## Closing note on positioning

The market is full of Next.js developers. It is **not** full of people who can hand a non-technical small business owner a website that maintains itself between major redesigns. That's the story to tell.

Lead every conversation with the unique offer (the phone-edit system + monthly improvement subscription), not the generic skill (modern websites). The first sells; the second is a commodity.
